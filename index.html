04 — The Pattern

Three traits every winner shares.

Across every company getting traction in this space, the same three characteristics show up. This is the filter any investor should apply to evaluate companies in this thesis.

01

Vertical-specific, not horizontal

Every winner starts with one industry's exact workflow, not a general automation platform. The depth of vertical knowledge creates a moat that horizontal tools can't replicate. Smartbase chose the manufacturing PO entry. Ruma Care chose infusion clinic prior auth. Specificity is the strategy.

02

Founder operated inside the problem

Smartbase founders slept at a factory. Ruma Care's co-founder quit his job to work as a medical assistant. Sidekick was built because the founder's father owns a manufacturing facility. The pattern is clear: the best founders in this space aren't researchers but operators who got tired of the problem and decided to fix it.

03

Sits on top of existing systems

No rip-and-replace. Every company here works with the ERP, the email, and the spreadsheet already in use. This dramatically lowers adoption friction and shortens sales cycles. The AI layer doesn't replace the system, but it makes the system intelligent. This also generates proprietary workflow data as a compounding moat over time.

The winning company won't be the one that builds the best AI. It'll be the one that figures out how to make a 40-person HVAC company run like it has a 10-person ops team.

05 — Why Now

This thesis needs an investor
who operates at the edge.

As AI gets embedded deeper into SMB and mid-market operations, security and governance become the critical next layer. The right investor brings more than capital, such as they bring a network that turns portfolio companies into distribution channels for each other.

Enterprise security network
As workflow AI touches sensitive ops data, financials, supply chain, patient records, security becomes mandatory. A portfolio with depth in enterprise security creates a direct distribution and partnership channel for companies operating in this space.
⬡
First-capital advantage
These companies are getting built right now at the pre-seed stage, in specific verticals, by operators who've been sitting on the problem for years. A first-check investor is perfectly matched to the stage where this thesis plays out — these companies are being built right now, by operators who've been sitting on the problem for years.
⬡
Deep advisory network
Former CTOs, CISOs, and operators from Fortune 100 companies — exactly the people who either buy these products or know someone who does. Portfolio company distribution through this kind of network is a structural advantage that compounds over time.
⬡
Healthcare & regulated verticals
A fund with existing exposure to regulated verticals such as healthcare, financial services, and insurance has a natural bridge to the most defensible sub-segment of this thesis and verticals where compliance, data sensitivity, and workflow complexity create the highest barriers to entry and the deepest moats.
